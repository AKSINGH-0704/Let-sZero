Starting from a blank page is one of the biggest reasons cold email drags on longer than it should. These two templates are starting points, not finished emails — they're built around the deliverability and personalization principles covered elsewhere in this Academy, so you're not fighting your own tools while you customize them.

## Why these two specifically

**Template 1** works when you have a real, specific trigger — funding news, a new hire, an expansion, something publicly true about the recipient right now. It leads with genuine personalization (see [personalizing cold email at scale](/repmail/learn/cold-email/personalize-cold-email-at-scale) for how to find and use these) rather than a generic opener.

**Template 2** is for when there's no obvious trigger to reference, but you have a clear, specific value proposition and know the kind of problem your product solves for companies like theirs. It leans on relevance and specificity instead of a personal hook.

## What makes both of these deliverability-safe

Neither template uses spam-trigger language — no "guaranteed," no excessive punctuation, no all-caps. Both are short enough to read easily on mobile, where most cold email actually gets opened first. Both end with a low-pressure ask rather than an aggressive one, which tends to perform better with cold (as opposed to warm) recipients. And critically, neither one is meant to be sent exactly as written to a large list — the bracketed sections need real, specific information per recipient, which is what keeps these from becoming the kind of obviously-templated email that underperforms regardless of how well the template itself was written.

## How to use these without losing the personal feel

Fill in every bracketed section with something genuinely true and specific before sending — a template with unfilled or vague placeholder text reads worse than no template at all. The words around the brackets are deliberately plain and human; resist the urge to make them sound more "professional" or salesy, since that's usually what tips a message from "a person wrote this" into "this is templated marketing."
