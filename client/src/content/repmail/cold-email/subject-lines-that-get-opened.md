If your open rates are low, the subject line is almost always the first place to look — it's the one thing every recipient sees before deciding whether to read another word.

## Why this matters more in cold email than anywhere else

In a reply to an existing thread, the subject line barely matters — the recipient already knows who you are. In cold email, the subject line is doing all the work: it's the only signal a stranger has to decide whether this is worth their attention or worth reporting as spam. It also matters to spam filters themselves, which weigh subject-line patterns (not just sender authentication) when deciding where a message lands.

## What actually gets emails opened

The subject lines that perform best in cold email tend to share a few traits: they're short (most inboxes truncate anything past 40-50 characters on mobile), they reference something specific and real about the recipient or their company rather than a generic pitch, and they read like something a real person would type, not like marketing copy. A subject line that could plausibly have come from a colleague tends to outperform one that clearly announces itself as an outreach email.

## What actively hurts you

Beyond simply being unpersuasive, certain patterns actively work against you — both with spam filters and with the recipient's own instincts. All-caps words, excessive exclamation points, and phrases like "guaranteed" or "act now" are exactly the kind of language spam filters were trained on, and they signal "sales email" to a human reader before they've even opened it. Overselling is its own separate problem: a subject line that promises something the email doesn't deliver gets you an open, but it costs you trust on the very first line — and trust is the entire game in cold email.

## The role of personalization

If you have a genuine, specific detail about the recipient or their company — something that shows you did real research, not a mail-merge field — using it in the subject line is one of the highest-leverage things you can do. It has to be real, though; a fake-sounding personalization token ("Hi {{firstName}}, quick question about {{company}}") reads worse than no personalization at all, because it signals the opposite of what you're going for.

Earning the open is only half the job — the first line of the body has to keep the reader going.
