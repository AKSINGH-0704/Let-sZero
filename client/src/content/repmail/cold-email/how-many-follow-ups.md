Most cold email replies don't come from the first message — they come from a follow-up. The question isn't really whether to follow up, it's how many times, and how far apart.

## Why most replies come later

A recipient not responding to your first email usually doesn't mean they're not interested — more often, it means the email arrived at a bad moment, got buried, or simply wasn't read closely enough for them to reply immediately. A well-spaced sequence gives you multiple chances to land at a moment when they actually have time to respond, without requiring you to write more emails than is reasonable.

## A cadence that works

Four total touches — the initial email plus three follow-ups — is a reasonable default that balances persistence against annoyance. The table above lays out a practical spacing: a few days between the first and second message, a bit more room before the third, and roughly a week before the final one. Spacing them too closely reads as pushy; spacing them too far apart loses the thread of context from the original email.

## What each follow-up should actually say

The second email shouldn't just say "just checking in" — that adds no new information and gives the recipient no additional reason to respond. It's more effective as a short note from a genuinely different angle: a different benefit, a different use case, or a quick, specific reason you're following up. The third email is a good place to add real value — a relevant resource, a specific insight about their industry, or social proof (a comparable company you've helped) that wasn't in the original message. 

## Why the last email often performs best

The final "breakup" email — something like "I'll assume the timing isn't right and won't follow up again" — consistently gets some of the highest reply rates in a sequence. It removes pressure rather than adding it, which paradoxically makes it easier for someone who was mildly interested but hadn't gotten around to replying to finally respond. It also gives you a clean, honest way to close out the sequence rather than trailing off indefinitely.
