"Personalized" cold email has a bad reputation, mostly because most of what gets called personalization is really just a first-name mail-merge token dropped into an otherwise generic template. Recipients can tell the difference immediately, and it often does more harm than sending no personalization at all — because it signals effort that wasn't real.

## What personalization actually needs to do

Real personalization isn't about how many variables you fill in — it's about demonstrating that you looked at this specific person or company before writing to them. A single genuine, specific observation ("I saw you're hiring for a Head of Sales role" or "congrats on the Series A") does more work than five generic merge fields, because it's the kind of detail a template can't fake convincingly.

## The structure that scales

The reason personalization usually breaks down at volume is that writing a fully custom email to every recipient doesn't scale, but generic templates don't convert. The middle path that actually works is a fixed structure with one or two genuinely variable, researched slots — not a template with your name swapped in, and not a fully bespoke email either. The template asset above shows this shape: most of the email is a consistent, well-written structure, and the personalization lives in one specific, real detail per recipient (a trigger event, a role they're hiring for, something concrete) rather than being spread thin across generic fields.

## What to actually research

The highest-leverage personalization details tend to be things that change: a company recently raising funding, expanding into a new market, hiring for a specific role, or publishing something publicly (a blog post, a press release, a job listing). These are concrete, verifiable, and specific enough that a recipient can tell you actually looked, without requiring a deep research process per email.

## Where AI-assisted drafting fits

Using AI to help draft the surrounding structure, or to help find and summarize a genuine trigger event, is a reasonable way to make this scale without sacrificing quality — as long as the specific detail itself is real and the final message still reads like something a person actually wrote and meant. The goal is never to automate the appearance of personalization; it's to make genuine personalization efficient enough to do at real volume.
